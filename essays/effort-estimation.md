## Introduction
In the world of project management, an estimate is more than just a guess, it is a hypothesis of how we believe time and effort will behave. However, as the saying goes, "life is what happens when you’re busy making other plans." This reflection examines the discrepancy between my initial effort estimates and the gritty reality of project execution. By analyzing the historical data used to build my baseline, I aim to uncover how the discipline of estimation transforms a chaotic workload into a manageable journey. Through this process, I have learned that the true value of tracking effort lies not in being "right," but in becoming more aware of the invisible complexities that define a successful project.

## 1. Estimation Methodology and Basis
My effort estimates were primarily based on historical data from previous issues, experiences done in the ICS 314 curriculum, and similar work done in my job as a mobile developer. I broke my issues down into small components assigned a complexity estimate to each based on my prior experiences. I used a "Complexity Multiplier" for the basis. I categorized tasks as Simple (0.5 hours), Medium (1 hours), or Complex (1.5+ hours).

## 2. The Value of Inaccurate Estimates
While my estimates were frequently off—often by as much as 20%, the act of estimating in advance was highly beneficial.

Defining Scope: Estimating forced me to visualize the steps required to complete a task. For example, estimating the "Deploy to Vercel" task helped me realize I hadn’t accounted for setting up the environment variables, which I then added to the plan.

Prioritization: It helped me identify "high-risk" tasks early. Even if the hours were wrong, I knew which tasks were daunting and which were easier, allowing me to tackle the hardest work while I had the most energy.

## 3. Utility of Tracking Actual Effort
Tracking actual effort was the most critical part of the process for informing future decisions.

Example: I noticed that "Debugging" consistently took longer than "Initial Coding." This data informed a decision to push the course map, a "nice-to-have" feature, to a later milestone ensure the core functionality was stable.

Future Planning: It highlighted my "optimism bias," teaching me to add a 20% buffer to all future estimates.

## 4. Tracking Tools, Methods, and Accuracy
Method/Tools: I used a stopwatch and the comments on our GitHub issue board to log "Estimated vs. Actual" hours while working.

Accuracy: I believe my tracking was roughly 80% accurate. While I logged time daily, I occasionally forgot to account for the non-coding effort required for these issues, such as coming up with the test cases for the playwright testing.

5. Reflections for Improvement
Next time, I would be more consistent with tracking my effort while working rather than attempting to make an estimate after completing the task, which is what I had to do for some of my issues. This would provide a more realistic range and reduce the stress caused by trying to make an authentic, unbiased guess after the issue had been completed.
